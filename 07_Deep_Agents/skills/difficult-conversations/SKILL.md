# Difficult Conversations Skill

You are helping prepare for a difficult conversation. Follow these steps:

## Step 1: Understand the Situation
Gather context about:
- Who is the conversation with (relationship)
- What is the core issue
- What triggered the need for this conversation
- What emotions are involved (yours and theirs)
- What is at stake

## Step 2: Clarify Your Goals
Define desired outcomes:
- What do you want to accomplish?
- What do you want them to understand?
- What relationship outcome do you want?
- What are you willing to compromise on?

## Step 3: Prepare Your Opening
Create an opening statement that:
- Is non-accusatory (use "I" statements)
- States the purpose clearly
- Invites dialogue
- Sets a collaborative tone

Example: "I'd like to talk about [topic] because [reason]. I value our relationship and want to understand your perspective."

## Step 4: Anticipate Responses
Prepare for:
- Defensive reactions
- Emotional responses
- Counter-arguments
- Unexpected perspectives
- Attempts to change the subject

## Step 5: Active Listening Framework
Plan to demonstrate understanding:
- Paraphrase what you hear
- Ask clarifying questions
- Acknowledge their feelings
- Look for common ground
- Avoid interrupting

## Step 6: Key Phrases to Use
Prepare helpful phrases:
- "Help me understand..."
- "What I'm hearing is..."
- "From your perspective..."
- "I feel [emotion] when [situation] because [reason]"
- "What would work better for you?"

## Step 7: Phrases to Avoid
Be aware of escalating language:
- "You always..." / "You never..."
- "You make me feel..."
- "That's not true"
- "You should..."
- Sarcasm or dismissive tones

## Step 8: Plan for Closure
Prepare to end constructively:
- Summarize agreements
- Clarify next steps
- Express appreciation for the dialogue
- Schedule follow-up if needed

## Step 9: Save Outputs
Write conversation prep to `workspace/difficult_conversation_prep.md`.

## Self-Care Reminders
- It's okay to take a break if emotions escalate
- You can ask to continue later
- Both perspectives can be valid
- Perfect outcomes are rare; progress is success
